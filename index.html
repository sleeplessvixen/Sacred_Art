Welcome to Our Collection

Discover our carefully curated collection of sacred artworks that span centuries of religious devotion and artistic mastery. Each piece tells a unique story of faith, craftsmanship, and spiritual significance.

Christ and Saint John the Evangelist

This emotionally resonant sculpture, made of polychromed and gilded oak sometime between 1470 and 1480, depicts Saint John the Evangelist resting on Christ's shoulder during the Last Supper. This type of devotional sculpture was popular in Swabia, a region on the Swiss border, and may have adorned the chapels of Dominican convents. It reflects the mysticism and contemplation of Christ's Passion that were prevalent in the late medieval period.

Enthroned Virgin with the Writing Christ Child

This limestone sculpture, dating from around 1480, showcases the Virgin Mary seated on an ornate pillow, with the Christ Child writing in an open book on her lap. This theme, which emerged in the late 15th century, symbolizes Mary’s role in educating Christ and foreshadows his future as a teacher. The sculpture's small size and uncarved back suggest it was intended for a small, intimate space, like a chapel or oratory.

Vesperbild (Pietà)

Germany, Upper Bavaria, 16th century
Lindenwood, polychromed and gilded

Overall: 89.1 x 78.7 x 32.4 cm (35 1/16 x 31 x 12 3/4 in.)

This poignant sculpture of the Virgin Mary cradling the dead body of Christ, made of polychromed and gilded lindenwood between 1500 and 1520, is known as a Vesperbild in German. It refers to the sculpture's use in the Good Friday evening prayer (vespers). The Master of Rabenden, the anonymous artist who created this piece, is named after his most famous work, the high altar of the church of Rabenden in Bavaria. This Vesperbild, like others of its kind, encouraged viewers to reflect on Christ's suffering and Mary's grief.
